# The Mechanism of Directionally Selective Units in Rabbit's Retina

## Metadata

* **File**: `files/barlow_1965_ds-units-rabbit-retina.pdf`
* **Published**: 1965
* **Authors**: H. B. Barlow, W. R. Levick
* **Venue**: The Journal of Physiology, 178(3):477-504
* **DOI**: `10.1113/jphysiol.1965.sp007638`

## Abstract

Directionally selective single units in the retinae of rabbits respond vigorously to a stimulus
object moved through the receptive field in one (preferred) direction, but give little or no
response to motion in the reverse (null) direction. The present paper investigates the mechanism of
this directional selectivity by showing: (1) it is not caused by optical aberrations or progressive
latency differences across the receptive field; (2) the directionally selective property resides in
small subunits distributed across the entire receptive field, each subtending 0.25-0.5 degrees; (3)
these subunits discriminate the sequence of excitation of pairs of neighbouring retinal regions; (4)
the sequence-discrimination is based primarily on an inhibitory mechanism that vetoes responses to
null sequences, rather than on the detection of excitatory conjunctions.

## Overview

Barlow and Levick address the mechanistic basis of direction selectivity in on-off retinal ganglion
cells of the rabbit, building on their prior discovery of DS units (Barlow and Hill, 1963; Barlow,
Hill and Levick, 1964). DS ganglion cells fire vigorously for preferred-direction motion but are
nearly silent for null-direction motion. This asymmetry is independent of stimulus contrast and
cannot be predicted from the static on/off receptive field map.

The paper proceeds through a systematic series of exclusions and positive demonstrations. Optical
aberrations are excluded: DS persists across more than 10 dioptres of refractive error and is
unaffected by stopping down the pupil to 1.25 mm. Differential response latencies are excluded: no
systematic gradient is found across the receptive field (Fig. 2). With these alternatives ruled out,
the paper establishes the mechanism positively.

Single-slit experiments show that the complete DS mechanism resides in subunits of 6-24 arc-minutes,
replicated uniformly across the full 3-4.5 degree receptive field. Two-spot temporal sequence
experiments establish that these subunits discriminate the temporal order of activation of two
neighbouring regions: the preferred-order stimulus (A then B) elicits more spikes than the
null-order stimulus (B then A), but only when the two regions are within about 24 arc-minutes.
Critically, null-sequence spike counts are consistently below the sum of individual responses,
confirming active inhibitory suppression.

Barlow1965 introduced the inhibitory veto model of DS computation. The specific assignment of the
inhibitory role to horizontal cells was later revised (starburst amacrine cells are the accepted
substrate), but the logical architecture has proven correct and remains the conceptual foundation of
the field, with 1,284 citations as of 2026.

## Architecture, Models and Methods

**Preparation.** Recordings used decerebrate or lightly urethane/chloralose anaesthetised rabbits
immobilised by continuous intravenous infusion of gallamine triethiodide (Flaxedil). Fine tungsten
electrodes recorded extracellular single-unit action potentials in the unopened eye. Only on-off DS
units were studied; on-type DS units were noted to have a different mechanism and excluded.

**Stimulus delivery.** Receptive fields were mapped at 57 cm using stationary spots (1 degree
diameter, 12 cd/m2, background 0.6 cd/m2). Moving stimuli used black-and-white cards behind
apertures, glow modulator tubes, and Perspex light pipes. For two-spot experiments, a custom
apparatus allowed two strip stimuli (0.1 x 2 degrees each) to be placed within 0.01 degrees of each
other and turned on and off independently in either order.

**Single-slit experiment (Table 1, Fig. 5).** A black-white border was moved through the receptive
field with its visible extent restricted by a fixed slit of variable width (8 arc-minutes to 1
degree 36 arc-minutes along the preferred-null axis). Responses graded 0-6. Ten units studied.
Threshold slit width for distinguishable preferred vs. null responses ranged from **6 to 24
arc-minutes** across units, establishing that a complete DS subunit spans 0.25-0.5 degrees.

**Two-slit sequence experiment (Table 2, Fig. 6).** Two 6-arc-minute slits at variable separations
(6 arc-minutes to 1 degree 36 arc-minutes along the preferred-null axis) illuminated in preferred
(AB) or null (BA) sequence. Preferred/null discrimination present up to approximately 24
arc-minutes; absent at 1 degree 6 arc-minutes and above.

**Inhibition quantification (Table 3).** Two slits presented singly and in null or preferred
sequence. Responses averaged over 4-7 repetitions counted within 0.5 sec. At 8 arc-minute
separation, off stimulus: A = 9.1, B = 3.2, A+B = 13.2; null = 8.3 spikes (deficit: 4.9); preferred
= 17.7 spikes (excess: 4.5). This design separates the inhibitory and facilitatory components.

**Controls.** Optical: DS persists across more than 10 dioptres refractive error; pupil stop-down to
1.25 mm does not abolish DS. Latency: no systematic on/off latency trend across the field. Speed: DS
fails near 0.7 degrees/sec (Fig. 9); optimal near 5 degrees/sec. Intermittent motion (Fig. 8):
bursts escape at each restart after 0.5 sec pauses, confirming inhibitory decay.

**Proposed circuit (Fig. 11).** Horizontal cells conduct laterally in the null direction via
telodendria, inhibiting bipolar cells and preventing null sequence responses. Ganglion cells pool
from bipolars responsive to preferred-direction sequences. Amacrine cells mediate surround
inhibition. The horizontal cell assignment was later revised to starburst amacrine cells.

## Results

* **DS persists across more than 10 dioptres** of refractive error in either direction; pupil
  stop-down to **1.25 mm** does not abolish DS (Fig. 1).
* **No latency gradient** across the receptive field for on or off responses (Fig. 2).
* **Subunit size**: threshold slit width for DS discrimination ranged from **6 to 24 arc-minutes**
  across 10 units; the DS mechanism resides within approximately **0.25-0.5 degrees** of retina.
* **Sequence interaction range**: preferred/null discrimination present at separations up to
  approximately **24 arc-minutes**; absent at **1 degree 6 arc-minutes** (Table 2).
* **Null inhibition quantified (8 arc-minutes, off, Table 3)**: A = 9.1, B = 3.2, A+B = 13.2; null =
  **8.3 spikes** (deficit 4.9); preferred = **17.7 spikes** (excess 4.5).
* **Null inhibition is the dominant effect**: null-sequence deficit present in every condition;
  preferred facilitation is present but smaller and less consistent.
* **Inhibition-free rim**: approximately **1 degree** zone at the preferred-direction leading edge
  responds to both motion directions, consistent with the inhibitory scheme.
* **Speed threshold**: DS breaks down at approximately **0.7 degrees/sec**; optimal near **5
  degrees/sec** (Fig. 9).
* **Intermittent null motion** elicits bursts at each restart after approximately 0.5 sec pause
  (Fig. 8), confirming inhibitory decay.
* **Surround inhibition**: moving edge across centre plus surround: **30 impulses** vs. **62
  impulses** with surround masked off (Fig. 10).
* **Receptive field diameter**: approximately **3-4.5 degrees** for the units studied.

## Innovations

### Inhibitory Veto Model of Direction Selectivity

Barlow and Levick introduced the inhibitory veto (and-not) model: null-direction responses are
actively suppressed by a laterally-conducting inhibitory interneuron. The null-sequence spike
deficit in Table 3 was the first direct quantitative evidence for inhibition as the mechanism,
distinguishing it from the Reichardt excitatory correlator. The logical structure has proven correct
even though the specific cell type was revised from horizontal to starburst amacrine cell.

### Sequence-Discriminating Subunit Architecture

The paper established that DS resides in many spatially replicated subunits of 6-24 arc-minutes,
uniformly distributed across the full receptive field. This multi-subunit architecture directly
informs how inhibitory synapses should be distributed in compartmental models: they must cover the
entire dendritic arbor, not just the perisomatic region.

### Hierarchical Feature-Extraction Framework

The Discussion introduced a formal logical analysis (union and and-not operations across two
synaptic layers) connecting retinal physiology to machine pattern recognition, anticipating later
theoretical work on pooling and normalization in visual processing.

## Datasets

No computational datasets were used or released. All data are in vivo extracellular single-unit
recordings from rabbit retinal ganglion cells. Quantitative data comprise spike counts and response
grades (0-6 scale) in Tables 1-3 and the figures. No external datasets were used.

## Main Ideas

* **Null-direction suppression is active inhibition**: the null-sequence spike deficit (Table 3)
  confirms that inhibitory (GABA) input from the null side is the dominant mechanism. A
  compartmental model of a DS ganglion cell must implement asymmetric inhibitory synaptic drive to
  reproduce null-direction suppression.
* **DS is a distributed dendritic property**: every approximately 0.25-0.5 degree subregion
  independently performs sequence discrimination. Inhibitory synapses must be distributed across the
  entire dendritic arbor; the dendrites themselves participate in directional computation.
* **Effective inhibitory interaction range is approximately 0.25-1 degree**: the sequence effect
  vanishes beyond approximately 24 arc-minutes. This constrains the spatial scale of GABAergic
  inputs and the effective electrotonic length constant in the model.
* **Optimal wave speed is approximately 5 degrees/sec**: DS disappears at approximately 0.7
  degrees/sec. Parametric wave sweeps in simulation should span 0.5-20 degrees/sec.
* **Preferred-direction facilitation is secondary**: the preferred-sequence excess over A+B is real
  but smaller than the null deficit. The model should prioritise null-direction inhibition first.

## Summary

Barlow and Levick investigate the mechanism of direction selectivity in on-off retinal ganglion
cells of the rabbit. The central question is what intraretinal circuit produces the striking
asymmetry between vigorous preferred-direction responses and near-silence for null-direction motion,
given that this asymmetry cannot be explained by the static on/off receptive field map.

The experimental strategy combines systematic exclusion of alternative hypotheses with two-spot
temporal sequence experiments. Optical aberrations and latency gradients are ruled out first.
Single-slit experiments localise the full DS mechanism to subunits of 6-24 arc-minutes, replicated
uniformly across the 3-4.5 degree receptive field. Two-spot experiments show that these subunits
discriminate the temporal order of excitation of pairs of neighbouring regions, with the effect
present only within approximately 24 arc-minutes.

The key finding is that null-direction selectivity is produced by active inhibition: null sequences
elicit fewer spikes than the sum of individual responses (Table 3), while preferred sequences
produce a small facilitation. The inhibitory mechanism is proposed to arise from horizontal cells
conducting laterally in the null direction to veto bipolar responses. The specific cell-type
assignment was later revised to starburst amacrine cells, but the logical architecture has proven
correct.

For this project, Barlow1965 provides the primary behavioural benchmark for the compartmental model:
it must fire vigorously for preferred-direction waves and be nearly silent for null-direction waves,
with asymmetry arising from spatially distributed inhibitory GABA synaptic input across the
dendritic arbor. The inhibitory interaction range constraint (approximately 0.25-1 degree) bounds
the spatial scale of GABA inputs, and the optimal wave speed of approximately 5 degrees/sec sets the
target velocity for parametric sweeps in simulation.
