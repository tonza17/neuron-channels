# Wiring Specificity in the Direction-Selectivity Circuit of the Retina

## Metadata

* **File**: `files/briggman_2011_sbem.pdf`
* **Published**: 2011
* **Authors**: Kevin L. Briggman (DE), Moritz Helmstaedter (DE), Winfried Denk (DE)
* **Venue**: Nature, Vol. 471, pp. 183-188
* **DOI**: `10.1038/nature09818`

## Abstract

The proper connectivity between neurons is essential for the implementation of the algorithms used
in neural computations, such as the detection of directed motion by the retina. The analysis of
neuronal connectivity is possible with electron microscopy, but technological limitations have
impeded the acquisition of high-resolution data on a large enough scale. Here we show, using serial
block-face electron microscopy and two-photon calcium imaging, that the dendrites of mouse starburst
amacrine cells make highly specific synapses with direction-selective ganglion cells depending on
the ganglion cell preferred direction. Our findings indicate that a structural (wiring) asymmetry
contributes to the computation of direction selectivity. The nature of this asymmetry supports some
models of direction selectivity and rules out others. It also puts constraints on the developmental
mechanisms behind the formation of synaptic connections. Our study demonstrates how otherwise
intractable neurobiological questions can be addressed by combining functional imaging with the
analysis of neuronal connectivity using large-scale electron microscopy.

## Overview

This paper addresses a long-standing question in retinal neuroscience: is direction selectivity in
retinal ganglion cells (DSGCs) produced by an asymmetry in synaptic strength alone (within a
structurally symmetric circuit), or does it require an underlying structural (wiring) asymmetry?
Prior electrophysiology had established that inhibitory input DSGCs receive from starburst amacrine
cells (SACs) is spatially asymmetric -- stronger from SACs on the null side -- but it remained
unresolved whether this reflected more synaptic contacts from null-side dendrites or simply stronger
individual synapses.

To answer this, Briggman, Helmstaedter, and Denk combined two-photon calcium imaging (to
functionally identify each DSGC preferred direction) with serial block-face scanning electron
microscopy (SBEM) of the same retinal tissue. They imaged 634 somata in a 300 x 300 um region of the
mouse ganglion cell layer, identified 25 On-Off DSGCs, and acquired a 350 x 300 x 60 um SBEM volume
at 16.5 x 16.5 x 23 nm resolution. This allowed them to trace the dendritic skeletons of 6 DSGCs and
24 SACs and map all 831 putative SAC-to-DSGC synapses.

The central finding is stark: SAC dendrites synapse selectively onto DSGCs whose null direction is
antiparallel to the SAC dendrite orientation. A northward SAC branch predominantly synapses onto the
southward-preferring DSGC, even while its dendrites physically overlap with eastward- and
westward-preferring DSGCs. The mean dendrite angle relative to the null direction was 165.2 +/- 51.7
degrees across all 831 synapses. Null-side SAC somata contributed 524 synapses compared with only 41
from preferred-side somata; crucially, the determinant of synapse formation was the individual
dendrite angle, not the soma-to-soma axis. When the soma-soma axis was between 45 and 135 degrees
off the null direction, connected dendrites still ran 24.3 +/- 2.8 degrees closer to null,
confirming that each SAC dendritic branch acts as an independent computational unit in selecting
postsynaptic targets.

These results demonstrate that the direction-selectivity circuit has a structural basis: the
asymmetric inhibitory conductances measured electrophysiologically arise from an asymmetric number
of synaptic contacts, not merely from potentiation of existing symmetric connections. This ruling
out of purely synaptic-strength-based models implicates local dendritic geometry or
activity-dependent signalling during development.

## Architecture, Models and Methods

**Functional imaging.** A dark-adapted wild-type C57BL/6 mouse retina (P29) was bulk-electroporated
with Oregon Green 488 BAPTA-1 (OGB-1, 5 mM hexapotassium salt) to load the ganglion cell layer with
a calcium indicator. A custom two-photon microscope (~930 nm Ti:sapphire laser,
through-the-objective stimulation) was used. Sulphorhodamine 101 (0.5-1 uM) visualised vasculature.
Moving-bar stimuli (200 x 1000 um bars, 0.5 mm/s, eight directions 45 degrees apart, five
presentations each) characterised direction tuning. Scan parameters: 64 x 64 pixels at 7.8 Hz from
nine 100 x 100 um fields in a 3 x 3 mosaic (300 x 300 um total). Tuning curves were generated by
integrating area under calcium transients and normalising to the peak direction. Among 634 imaged
somata, 25 On-Off DSGCs were identified by clustering into four cardinal preferred-direction groups
(N, E, S, W).

**Tissue preparation.** Immediately after imaging, the retina was incubated in 5 mg/ml horseradish
peroxidase (HRP), fixed in 2% glutaraldehyde / 0.1 M cacodylate buffer, DAB-precipitated (5.6 mM
DAB, 1.4 mM H2O2, 8 h), stained with 2% osmium tetroxide, enhanced with en bloc lead citrate,
dehydrated in an ethanol series, and embedded in Epon Hard. A second retina (P30) was conventionally
stained with osmium-ferrocyanide, thiocarbohydrazide, osmium, uranyl acetate, and lead aspartate.
Both retinas were re-embedded on aluminium stubs (retinal plane vertical) and trimmed to ~200 x 400
um block faces.

**SBEM acquisition.** FEI QuantaFEG 200 SEM with custom back-scattered electron detector (AXUV
silicon diode) and custom microtome. Primary retina: 2.5 keV, ~230 pA, 1.9 us dwell, 16.5 x 16.5 nm
pixels, 23 nm sections, 50 Pa water vapour, 13,000 sections as a 7 x 13 mosaic; total volume ~60 x
350 x 300 um. Second retina: 2.0 keV, 12 nm pixels, 25 nm sections, high vacuum with 100-200 nm gold
coating, 5,760 sections; total ~50 x 65 x 145 um. Data were aligned by cross-correlation shift
vectors with a global least-squares fit and split into 128 x 128 x 128 voxel cubes for viewing in
KNOSSOS.

**Skeleton tracing and synapse annotation.** Dendritic skeletons (nodes every ~250 nm) were manually
traced in KNOSSOS. Six DSGC trees and 24 SAC trees (11 On, 13 Off) were reconstructed. Of 9,260
skeleton proximity events within 1.5 um, 831 varicose contacts were classified as putative synapses
and 2,650 as incidental contacts. All 43 varicose contacts inspected in the conventionally stained
dataset showed ultrastructural synapse hallmarks (vesicles, dense pre/postsynaptic membrane
specialisations).

**Statistical analysis.** For each synapse, the SAC dendrite angle was defined as the vector from
SAC soma to synapse location. The relative angle gamma = (theta_soma - theta_dendrite) *
sign(theta_soma - theta_ND) was computed; gamma < 0 indicates dendrites closer to null than the
soma-soma vector. Custom MATLAB routines were used throughout.

## Results

* SAC dendrite-to-null-direction mean angle: **165.2 +/- 51.7 degrees** (mean +/- s.d., n = 831
  synapses), showing most SAC inputs arise from null-antiparallel dendrites.
* Null-side SAC somata contributed **524 synapses** vs. only **41** from preferred-side somata
  (|theta_soma - theta_ND| < 45 vs. > 135 degrees): a **~12.8:1 ratio**.
* When soma-soma axis was 45-135 degrees off null, connected dendrites ran **24.3 +/- 2.8 degrees**
  (mean +/- s.e.m.) closer to null than the soma-soma vector.
* **75.2% of synapses** in the intermediate soma-angle range (45-135 degrees) had gamma < 0.
* **0 synaptic specialisations** found at incidental SAC-DSGC contacts in the conventionally stained
  dataset; all 43 varicose contacts showed synapse hallmarks.
* Total: **831 putative synapses** from 24 SACs onto 6 DSGCs annotated in the SBEM volume.
* SBEM primary volume: approximately **60 x 350 x 300 um** at 16.5 x 16.5 x 23 nm resolution.
* No detectable difference in wiring specificity between On and Off sublayers.

## Innovations

### Correlated Functional-Structural Connectome of the DS Circuit

This paper provided the first direct structural proof of wiring asymmetry in the DS circuit by
combining calcium imaging (to assign preferred directions) with SBEM of the identical tissue. The
approach -- functional imaging then connectomic EM of the same preparation -- became a paradigm for
circuit neuroscience (connectomics with functional context).

### Dendrite-Angle Rule: SAC Branch as Independent Synaptic Selector

Synapse formation depends on the orientation of the individual SAC dendritic branch, not simply
which side of the DSGC the SAC soma sits on. This identifies the SAC dendrite as the unit of wiring
specificity and implies a local geometry- or activity-dependent developmental mechanism, ruling out
cell-identity-level wiring rules.

### Structural Basis Rules Out Symmetric-Wiring Models

The ~12.8:1 null-to-preferred synapse count excess provides sufficient structural substrate to
explain the asymmetric inhibitory conductance measured electrophysiologically without invoking
selective potentiation of a symmetric substrate. Models based solely on asymmetric synaptic weights
on symmetric wiring are disfavoured.

### SBEM Surface-Labelling Protocol

The HRP/DAB surface-labelling method preferentially stains cell surfaces while leaving intracellular
content unstained, improving the ability to trace thin neurites through dense neuropil and enabling
reliable synapse classification at volume scale.

## Main Ideas

* **Structural wiring asymmetry is necessary**: The ~12.8:1 excess of null-side SAC synapses
  establishes that direction selectivity requires asymmetric inhibitory wiring. A compartmental DSGC
  model must incorporate spatially asymmetric inhibitory input placement -- not just conductance
  weight differences.
* **Dendrite orientation governs placement, not soma side**: Individual SAC branches oriented along
  the DSGC null direction selectively synapse onto that cell regardless of soma position. Inhibitory
  inputs should be distributed by alignment of the presynaptic SAC branch with the null axis, not
  simply clustered on the null-side half of the DSGC arbor.
* **Null-direction inhibition model is confirmed; push-pull is not**: Preferred-direction- oriented
  SAC contacts are very sparse, arguing against a cholinergic push-pull mechanism. The Barlow-Levick
  null-direction lateral inhibition account is structurally validated.
* **EM resolution required for reliable connectivity data**: Light-microscopy proximity studies gave
  contradictory results. Published EM wiring data should be taken as structural ground truth for
  inhibitory input placement in DSGC models.
* **DSGC skeletons as morphological reference**: The 6 reconstructed DSGC skeletons represent real
  mouse On-Off DSGC morphologies. Downstream papers from this group (Helmstaedter et al. 2013) may
  provide SWC-compatible morphologies suitable for NEURON compartmental models.

## Summary

Briggman, Helmstaedter, and Denk (2011) address whether direction selectivity in mouse On-Off DSGCs
requires a structural wiring asymmetry or merely functional differences in synapse strength within
an anatomically symmetric circuit. Prior patch-clamp studies had established that inhibitory input
from SACs is asymmetric -- stronger from null-side SACs -- but could not determine whether this
reflected more contacts or stronger individual synapses, nor whether SAC dendrite orientation rather
than soma location was the relevant factor.

The authors first used two-photon calcium imaging with OGB-1 to identify preferred directions of 25
On-Off DSGCs in a 300 x 300 um mouse retinal region, then fixed the identical tissue and acquired a
60 x 350 x 300 um SBEM volume at 16.5 x 16.5 x 23 nm resolution. Manual skeleton tracing in KNOSSOS
yielded 6 DSGC and 24 SAC dendritic trees; 831 putative SAC-to-DSGC synapses were annotated at
varicose contact sites and confirmed by ultrastructural criteria.

The data reveal a striking structural asymmetry: SAC dendrites oriented antiparallel to a DSGC
preferred direction preferentially form synapses with that cell (mean dendrite-to-null angle 165.2
+/- 51.7 degrees; 524 vs. 41 synapses from null vs. preferred-side somata, ~12.8:1). Critically,
when soma-soma axis and dendrite orientation conflict, dendrite orientation wins: connected
dendrites run 24.3 +/- 2.8 degrees closer to the null axis, confirming individual SAC branches as
independent synaptic selectors.

For this project compartmental DSGC model, these data establish the structural baseline for
inhibitory input placement: asymmetric by dendrite orientation, concentrated from null-direction SAC
branches distributed across the full DSGC dendritic field. Any model using spatially uniform
inhibitory placement or relying solely on weight asymmetry is inconsistent with this structural
evidence. The paper also directly cites Schachter et al. (2010), a computational DSGC model in which
dendritic spikes amplify SAC-derived inhibition, connecting this anatomy paper directly to the
biophysical modelling literature this project builds upon.
