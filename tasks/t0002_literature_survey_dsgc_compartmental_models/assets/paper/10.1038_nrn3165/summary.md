# Direction selectivity in the retina: symmetry and asymmetry in structure and function

## Metadata

* File: `files/vaney_2012_ds-retina-review.pdf`
* Published: 2012
* Authors: David I. Vaney 🇦🇺, Benjamin Sivyer 🇦🇺, W. Rowland Taylor 🇺🇸
* Venue: Nature Reviews Neuroscience, Vol. 13(3), pp. 194-208
* DOI: `10.1038/nrn3165`

## Abstract

Visual information is processed in the retina to a remarkable degree before it is transmitted to
higher visual centres. Several types of retinal ganglion cells (the output neurons of the retina)
respond preferentially to image motion in a particular direction, and each type of
direction-selective ganglion cell (DSGC) is comprised of multiple subtypes with different preferred
directions. The direction selectivity of the cells is generated by diverse mechanisms operating
within microcircuits that rely on independent neuronal processing in individual dendrites of both
the DSGCs and the presynaptic neurons that innervate them.

## Overview

This review consolidates a decade of morphological, physiological and developmental work on the
retinal direction-selective (DS) microcircuit. The authors begin from the Barlow-Hill-Levick
observation that direction selectivity is generated only two synapses downstream of the
photoreceptors, and walk the reader through the receptive-field properties of DSGCs, the subtype
taxonomy of On-Off and On DSGCs in rabbit and mouse retina, the synaptic wiring to starburst
amacrine cells (SACs), and the competing pre- and post-synaptic accounts of how the computation is
performed. The coverage spans four cardinal-direction On-Off DSGC subtypes in rabbit, the mouse
DRD4-, TRHR-, BD-, HB9- and W9-DSGC molecular subtypes, and the transient/sustained On DSGC split
projecting to the accessory optic system.

A central organising principle of the review is Barlow and Levick's three ingredients required by
every DS "subunit": a spatial asymmetry, a nonlinearity, and a time delay. The authors argue that
each ingredient maps onto a concrete cellular substrate - the asymmetry onto the null-side-biased
GABAergic wiring between SAC dendrites and DSGCs (confirmed by serial block-face electron microscopy
showing an ~11:1 null:preferred synapse ratio), the nonlinearities onto threshold processes in
neurotransmitter release, dendritic spike generation and somatic spiking, and the delay onto the
prolonged GABAergic inhibition released by SACs.

A second organising theme is the disagreement between pre- and post-synaptic accounts of DSGC
computation. Somatic voltage-clamp recordings show that excitatory currents are also larger in the
preferred direction, but the authors argue this may partly reflect voltage-clamp errors in the thin
distal dendrites, and they emphasise the post-synaptic contribution of dendritic spikes and local
shunting inhibition to the final spike output. The review makes this tension explicit and leaves it
open as a major unresolved question - directly relevant to any compartmental model of a DSGC.

## Architecture, Models and Methods

This is a review paper, so the "methods" are a synthesis of methods used across the primary
literature the authors cite. The key experimental and modelling techniques covered are: (i)
extracellular and intracellular spike recordings from DSGCs in rabbit and mouse retina, with
pharmacological dissection using GABA_A-receptor antagonists and nicotinic cholinergic antagonists;
(ii) dye filling (Neurobiotin, Lucifer Yellow) and tracer-coupling experiments for morphology and
mosaic analysis; (iii) transgenic labelling of molecularly defined DSGC subtypes in mouse (DRD4,
TRHR, BD, HB9/Mnx1, W9, CART, Cdh6, Col25a1, Mmp17 markers); (iv) paired patch-clamp recordings
between SACs and DSGCs under visual or channelrhodopsin-2 stimulation, with the DSGC clamped at
either the excitatory or inhibitory reversal potential to isolate GABAergic or cholinergic currents;
(v) two-photon calcium imaging of SAC dendrites with calcium-indicator dyes during apparent-motion
paradigms (test flash with centrifugal or centripetal conditioning flashes over the dendritic
arbor); (vi) large-scale population calcium imaging of a ganglion-cell-layer patch combined with
serial block-face scanning electron microscopy and neuronal reconstruction to map 25 DSGCs and 24
overlapping SACs and their putative synapses; (vii) developmental blockade experiments using
intravitreal injections of GABA_A agonists/antagonists, nicotinic antagonists and tetrodotoxin to
test whether neural activity sculpts the DS wiring.

On the modelling side the review discusses the passive cable properties of SAC dendrites as an
explanation for the centrifugal-centripetal calcium asymmetry, the proposal of a chloride gradient
along SAC dendrites from opposing chloride co-transporters (potentially shifting the GABA reversal
potential along the dendrite), and both the passive shunting-inhibition model and the active
dendritic-spike model for DSGC post-synaptic computation. Modelling results cited include the
demonstration that once initiated a dendritic spike is immune to shunting even by unreasonably large
intervening inhibitory inputs. No new simulations or hyperparameters are introduced by this review
itself.

## Results

Because this is a review, the "results" are the synthesis of quantitative findings the authors quote
from the primary literature.

* On-Off DSGCs tile the retina as **four subtypes** whose preferred directions align with the four
  cardinal ocular axes (anterior, posterior, superior, inferior); in rabbit the superior subtype
  shows tracer coupling and accounts for **~3% of all RGCs**, so the four subtypes together make up
  **~12% of all RGCs**.
* In mouse retina the DRD4-GFP line labels a posterior-preferring subtype at **~130 cells per mm^2**
  (~2,000 cells in a ~15 mm^2 retina, ~4% of RGCs), while TRHR-DSGCs are about half as dense;
  HB9-DSGCs are present at **~80 cells per mm^2**.
* DSGC dendritic fields in rabbit are strongly territorial (little overlap within subtype), while
  mouse DSGC dendritic fields extend as far as the soma of neighbouring cells of the same subtype.
* Paired recordings in rabbit retina show that depolarising a null-side SAC evokes a GABA_A-mediated
  inhibitory current in the DSGC that is **~9x larger** than that from a preferred-side SAC, whereas
  evoked cholinergic currents are **statistically indistinguishable** between null- and
  preferred-side SACs, indicating that the DS asymmetry lives in the inhibitory, not the excitatory,
  connection.
* Serial block-face EM reconstruction of six DSGCs and 24 overlapping SACs in mouse retina found
  **~11x more putative null-side than preferred-side SAC synapses** per DSGC, giving a structural
  substrate for the physiological asymmetry.
* Developmentally, SAC-to-DSGC inhibition is **symmetric at P6 and becomes ~3-fold asymmetric by the
  end of the second postnatal week**, driven primarily by an increase in null-side inhibitory
  strength; this transition happens without visual experience and survives intravitreal GABA_A
  agonist or antagonist, nicotinic agonist, or TTX blockade of retinal activity.
* Apparent-motion stimuli show that the DS subunit circuitry is responsive to image motion of less
  than **0.25 degrees (~40 micrometres on rabbit retina)**, with subunit replication at small
  intervals across a receptive field that spans about **3 degrees (~500 micrometres)**.
* Each DSGC dendrite is overlapped by **25-70 SACs in rabbit and ~30 SACs in mouse**, giving a very
  high substrate density for overlapping DS subunits.
* On DSGCs comprise a sustained and a transient type, each with three cardinal-direction subtypes;
  the superior On DSGCs (SPIG1-GFP) and inferior On DSGCs project to the medial terminal nucleus of
  the accessory optic system and drive the slow-velocity optokinetic reflex.
* GABAergic and cholinergic co-transmission from SACs have different calcium sensitivities and
  different N-type vs P/Q-type calcium-channel dependencies, consistent with distinct release
  machineries at the same SAC dendrites.
* Modelling indicates that once a DSGC dendritic spike is initiated it propagates faithfully to the
  soma and is **immune to shunting even by unreasonably large intervening inhibition**, supporting
  dendritic-spike-based subunit independence.

## Innovations

### Integrated microcircuit account of retinal DS

The review unifies presynaptic (SAC dendrite asymmetry, centrifugal release, reciprocal SAC-SAC
inhibition, chloride gradient) and postsynaptic (dendritic spikes, local shunting) mechanisms into
one coherent microcircuit diagram (the paper's Figure 6), with each of Barlow and Levick's three
computational ingredients mapped to a specific cellular process. This is the conceptual map the
field has used since.

### Explicit voltage-clamp-error critique of DS excitation

The authors propose a simple and skeptical alternative explanation for the apparent direction
selectivity of excitatory currents measured at the soma: that somatic voltage-clamp recordings on
electrotonically extended DSGC dendrites systematically under-estimate excitation in the null
direction because null-direction inhibition is larger and coincident with excitation there.
Inhibition-driven voltage-clamp errors could therefore masquerade as directional excitation. This
reframes a large body of published evidence and is directly relevant to any model calibrated against
somatic voltage-clamp measurements.

### Structural-physiological synthesis via EM + calcium imaging

The review highlights the Briggman, Helmstaedter and Denk 2011 study that combined two-photon
calcium imaging of 25 functionally classified DSGCs with serial block-face electron microscopy to
directly count null-side vs preferred-side SAC synapses onto six reconstructed DSGCs. The ~11:1
null:preferred synapse ratio turned a hypothesis about wiring asymmetry into a measured structural
fact.

### Molecular-subtype taxonomy of DSGCs

The review consolidates the mouse genetic-marker explosion (DRD4, TRHR, BD, HB9/Mnx1, W9, SPIG1,
CART, Cdh6, Col25a1, Mmp17) into a coherent subtype-by-preferred-direction table across On-Off and
On DSGCs, and discusses criteria for calling two marker-defined populations the same vs different
subtypes (mosaic regularity, dendritic-field coverage, co-labelling). This sets the template for how
DSGC subtype work has been organised since.

## Datasets

This is a review paper; no datasets were used or released. The review synthesises published
experimental data from third-party studies on rabbit, rat and mouse retinal preparations (dye fills,
paired recordings, calcium imaging, SBF-EM reconstructions, transgenic labelling). Readers wanting
the underlying data must follow the ~134 citations to the primary literature.

## Main Ideas

* For a DSGC compartmental model targeting a preferred-vs-null angle-to-spike-frequency curve, the
  dominant direction-selective input is asymmetric GABAergic inhibition from null-side SAC dendrites
  (~9x larger conductance, ~11x more synapses than preferred side); cholinergic excitation from SACs
  is effectively symmetric and should be modelled as isotropic co-activation.
* Somatic voltage-clamp measurements of DSGC excitatory currents likely over-estimate directional
  asymmetry in excitation due to space-clamp errors in the thin distal dendrites; model calibration
  should prefer inhibitory-current measurements and treat reported excitatory directionality
  cautiously or as a free parameter, consistent with the project's goal of varying AMPA vs GABA
  input density and kinetics.
* DSGC dendritic spikes (voltage-gated, self-propagating) are a plausible post-synaptic substrate
  for the project's Research Question 4 on the effect of dendritic voltage-gated conductances: an
  active dendrite with a local spike threshold implements a per-subunit nonlinearity that is robust
  to intervening inhibition, which a passive dendrite cannot reproduce.
* The review fixes the biophysical geometry the project should adopt: bistratified On-Off DSGC
  morphology, dendritic-field diameter ~150-200 micrometres per sublamina, ~25-70 overlapping SACs
  per dendrite in rabbit (~30 in mouse), and subunit spacing of ~40 micrometres - these numbers
  constrain sensible values for compartment density, synaptic-input density and the spatial sampling
  of the excitatory/inhibitory wave stimuli.
* A credible target tuning curve for the project should treat asymmetric inhibition as the primary
  shaper of the angle-response curve and dendritic spikes as the mechanism that makes each subunit
  report to the soma independently; morphology-driven sharpening (active dendrites, branching
  pattern) should be investigated against this backbone.

## Summary

This 2012 Nature Reviews Neuroscience article by Vaney, Sivyer and Taylor is the standard decadal
synthesis of retinal direction selectivity. Its research question is how the retina - within two
synapses of the photoreceptors - produces ganglion cells whose spike output prefers one direction of
image motion, and in particular how the three Barlow-Levick computational ingredients (spatial
asymmetry, nonlinearity, time delay) are implemented at the cellular level. The scope covers On-Off
DSGCs, On DSGCs and Off DSGCs in rabbit, rat and mouse, with the microcircuit-level focus on
starburst amacrine cells and bipolar cells as the presynaptic substrate and on DSGC dendrites as the
postsynaptic substrate.

The synthesis is built from four data streams. Morphological work (dye fills, SBF-EM,
Neurobiotin-tracer coupling) defines the bistratified DSGC dendritic geometry and the SAC mosaic.
Pharmacology (GABA_A and nicotinic antagonists) and paired cell recordings pin down which
transmitters and which sides of the SAC carry the directional signal. Calcium imaging of SAC
dendrites under apparent-motion paradigms establishes that individual SAC dendrites are themselves
DS units whose calcium transients are biased toward centrifugal motion. Developmental experiments
(intravitreal GABA and cholinergic drugs, dark-rearing, TTX) show that the direction-selective
wiring is essentially hard-wired.

The headline findings are that null-side SAC-to-DSGC GABAergic inhibition is **~9x larger** in
conductance and **~11x more numerous** in synapses than the preferred-side input, that cholinergic
SAC-to-DSGC excitation is spatially symmetric, and that DSGC dendrites carry voltage-gated
self-propagating dendritic spikes that are immune to shunting by intervening inhibition once
initiated. The authors also raise a provocative methodological caveat - that reported directional
asymmetries in DSGC excitatory currents may reflect somatic voltage-clamp errors on electrotonically
extended dendrites rather than genuine DS glutamatergic or cholinergic inputs. This leaves the
relative pre- vs post-synaptic contributions to the final spike output partially unresolved.

For this project the review is foundational. It fixes the cell type (On-Off DSGC), the minimum
biophysical geometry (bistratified dendrites, ~150-200 micrometre dendritic field per sublamina, ~40
micrometre subunit spacing), the dominant directional input (asymmetric null-side GABAergic
inhibition, symmetric cholinergic and glutamatergic excitation), and the post-synaptic nonlinearity
(dendritic spikes). It directly informs Research Question 1 (somatic Na/K conductance combinations
must still support spiking under large null-direction inhibitory conductances), Research Question 3
(AMPA/GABA input density should be calibrated against the ~9:1 null/preferred IPSC amplitude and
isotropic EPSC), and Research Question 4 (active vs passive dendrites should be compared against the
dendritic-spike substrate the authors endorse). Its voltage-clamp-error argument is a direct caution
against over-fitting to published excitatory-current directionality.
