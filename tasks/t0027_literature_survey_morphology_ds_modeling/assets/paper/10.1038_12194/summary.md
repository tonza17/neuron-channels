# Dendritic asymmetry cannot account for directional responses of neurons in visual cortex

## Metadata

* **File**: `files/anderson_1999_dendritic-asymmetry-v1-ds.pdf`
* **Published**: 1999-09-01
* **Authors**: J. C. Anderson 🇨🇭, T. Binzegger 🇨🇭, O. Kahana 🇮🇱, K. A. C. Martin
  🇨🇭, I. Segev 🇮🇱
* **Venue**: Nature Neuroscience, volume 2, issue 9, pp. 820-824
* **DOI**: `10.1038/12194`

## Abstract

A simple model was proposed to account for the direction selectivity of neurons in the primary
visual cortex, area V1. In this model, the temporal asymmetries in the summation of inhibition and
excitation that produce directionality were generated by structural asymmetries in the tangential
organization of the basal dendritic tree of cortical neurons. We reconstructed dendritic trees of
neurons with known direction preferences and found no correlation between the small biases of a
neuron’s dendritic morphology and its direction preference. Detailed simulations indicated that
even when the electrotonic asymmetries in the dendrites were extreme, as in cortical Meynert cells,
the biophysical properties of single neurons could contribute only partially to the directionality
of cortical neurons.

## Overview

**Cortical flag**: cat V1 simple cells (plus monkey Meynert cells in simulation). This paper
provides a NEGATIVE result — dendritic asymmetry is not sufficient to explain V1 direction
selectivity, contradicting the morphology-causal story established for retinal starburst amacrine
cells and ON-OFF direction-selective ganglion cells. It therefore acts as the cortical counterpoint
in the morphology-versus-DS literature.

The paper is a direct experimental and computational test of Livingstone’s (1998) hypothesis that
the direction preference of primary-visual-cortex (V1) neurons is generated by tangential
asymmetries of their basal dendritic tree acting as a delay line (excitation from tip to soma
summates temporally in the preferred direction; excitation from soma outward is intercepted by
somatic inhibition in the null direction). Anderson and colleagues reconstructed 32 physiologically
characterized cat V1 neurons of different classes (layer 3-6 pyramids, layer 4 spiny stellates and
star pyramids, interneurons) in three dimensions, projected them onto the retinotopic tangential
surface of V1, and quantified the angular offset between each neuron’s preferred direction of
motion and the direction of its dendritic mass bias.

Two main findings emerge. First, experimentally, the distribution of angular differences between
preferred direction and the dendritic bias angle is statistically indistinguishable from a uniform
random distribution (Kolmogorov-Smirnov p = 0.23), and most neurons exhibit only weak asymmetries;
the hypothesis predicts a sharp peak at 180 degrees, which is not observed. Second, computationally,
the authors built a detailed NEURON compartmental model of reconstructed Meynert cells — the one
cortical cell class with genuinely extreme dendritic asymmetry and long basal dendrites (up to 770
micrometres) — and even under maximally favourable conditions the single-neuron mechanism supports
direction selectivity only at unphysiologically fast stimulus velocities (50-100 degrees/s) and
quickly degrades when inputs are mapped onto more than one dendrite. The authors conclude that
cortical direction selectivity must be computed largely by network mechanisms (intracortical
recurrent excitation and inhibition, delay lines from lagged LGN inputs), not by single-cell
dendritic geometry.

## Architecture, Models and Methods

**Experimental protocol.** 32 single neurons in cat primary visual cortex were first recorded
extracellularly and their receptive fields were mapped by hand for orientation, size, binocularity
and direction preference; each cell was classified as non-directional (similar forward/backward
responses), direction-preferential (biased) or direction-selective (response ratio > 3 as per
Livingstone 1998). The classification was cross-checked against a quantitative direction index DI =
(pref - nonpref)/(pref + nonpref) on 23 additional cells; hand-classified directional cells had DI
in the range 0.5-1.0. Cells were then re-mapped intracellularly and filled with horseradish
peroxidase (HRP). Tissue was osmicated and resin-embedded to eliminate differential shrinkage, which
is critical for quantitative morphology. Three extra Meynert cells (two monkey, one cat) were
retrogradely filled with biocytin for simulation-only use. Reconstructions were made in three
dimensions with an in-house computer-assisted microscope (TRAKA).

**Morphology analysis.** Each reconstruction was rotated into the x-z (tangential) plane, corrected
14 degrees for the mean offset between the stereotaxic and retinotopic coordinate systems of cat
area 17, and projected onto the cortical surface. A polar scan at 1-degree steps computed, for each
angle Phi, the summed dendritic length R lying in the distal third of a 30-degree sector; the angle
Phi_max of the maximum sector defined the dendritic bias. The paper tests the angular offset between
Phi_max and the preferred direction against a uniform null, and also reports the asymmetry magnitude
as the difference in R between opposite sectors.

**Compartmental simulations.** Simulations used NEURON (Hines and Carnevale 1997). The Meynert-cell
tree was discretized so that each compartment was at most 0.1 lambda. 2,000 AMPA-like excitatory
synapses were placed uniformly along the longest basal dendrite at 1 synapse per 1.5 micrometre
(alpha-synapse, g_peak = 0.4 nS, t_peak = 0.3 ms, E_rev = 0 mV) and activated sequentially either
distal-to-proximal (D-P, preferred) or proximal-to-distal (P-D, null) over a variable "sweep
duration" (proxy for stimulus velocity). GABA_A-like somatic inhibition (tau_1 = 0.5 ms, tau_2 = 7.5
ms, tau_3 = 33 ms, E_rev = -70 mV, peak conductance matched to the cell’s input conductance) was
activated 2 ms after the closest excitatory synapse. Membrane parameters: C_m = 1 uF/cm^2, R_i = 150
Ohm-cm, R_m = 5e3 or 5e4 Ohm-cm^2 giving tau_m = 5 ms or 50 ms respectively. A two-dendrite control
mapped the same input density onto the opposite basal dendrite as well.

## Results

* Angular offset between preferred direction of motion and dendritic bias angle (Phi_max) for the 32
  cat V1 neurons is indistinguishable from uniform random: **Kolmogorov-Smirnov p = 0.23**; the
  Livingstone hypothesis predicts a peak at **180 degrees** that is not observed.
* Changing bin sizes or measuring from inner, middle or total dendritic thirds does not change the
  random distribution — the null result is robust to the asymmetry metric chosen.
* Most of the 32 reconstructed neurons show only small dendritic asymmetries; only **2 of 32**
  neurons have their longest-dendrite direction aligned with 180 degrees from preferred, and such
  matches are consistent with chance.
* In the most favourable single-dendrite Meynert simulation, peak somatic EPSP is obtained at a
  sweep duration of **10 ms** along the **770 micrometre** longest basal dendrite, giving an optimal
  preferred velocity of **77 mm/s** on cortex, i.e., **~77 degrees/s** of visual field at cat
  area-centralis magnification (1 degree/mm).
* The analytically expected propagation velocity **2 lambda / tau_m** = 2 x 188 um / 5 ms = **75
  mm/s** matches the simulated optimum of 77 mm/s, confirming the model is governed by cable
  propagation of passive EPSPs.
* Measured cat V1 velocity tuning peaks at roughly **20 degrees/s** (Orban, Kennedy, Maes 1981) and
  Livingstone’s monkey V1 data peaks near **10 degrees/s** — an order of magnitude below the ~77
  degrees/s required by the single-dendrite mechanism at realistic tau_m = 5 ms.
* Increasing tau_m from 5 ms to 50 ms shifts the optimal single-dendrite velocity down to about **24
  mm/s** (close to observed tuning), but this requires an unphysically long membrane time constant
  in vivo and simultaneously shrinks the D-P versus P-D difference.
* Adding synapses onto an opposing basal dendrite (the two-dendrite case, closer to real input
  statistics) destroys the velocity peak — the velocity profile becomes a featureless high-pass
  curve and the preferred/null voltage difference collapses; a perfectly symmetric tree yields zero
  directionality.
* When integrated synaptic charge (not peak voltage) is used as the output variable — more
  relevant for spontaneously firing in vivo neurons — the D-P versus P-D difference is small
  across all velocities, further weakening any dendritic contribution.

## Innovations

### Direct experimental falsification of the dendritic-delay-line model for V1

This is the first published quantitative test of the Livingstone (1998) morphology-causal hypothesis
for V1 direction selectivity in which direction preference and three-dimensional dendritic
morphology are measured in the same cells. The sample (32 cat V1 neurons with HRP fills, covering
layers 3-6 and multiple cell classes) enables a statistical test of the predicted 180-degree
alignment, which is rejected.

### Tangential-projection morphometry pipeline

The authors formalize a reproducible pipeline — osmication plus resin embedding to remove
shrinkage bias, tangential re-projection of the reconstruction, 14-degree correction to align with
cat-V1 retinotopy, and a polar dendritic-length scan with variable radial cut-off — that cleanly
separates genuine tangential mass bias from noise due to short dendrites. This framework is directly
reusable for morphology-DS analyses in other systems (including retinal DSGC and starburst amacrine
cells).

### Compartmental model of cortical Meynert cells including directionality

The Meynert-cell NEURON model (ModelDB 3812) was the first detailed cortical-pyramidal model
specifically built to test the single-cell dendritic-asymmetry hypothesis for direction selectivity,
including distributed AMPA excitation, a somatic GABA_A synapse with realistic triple-exponential
kinetics from Galarreta and Hestrin (1997), and an explicit parameterization of sweep velocity that
maps onto visual-space motion via cortical magnification.

### Analytical link between cable propagation and velocity tuning

The paper ties the simulated optimal sweep velocity directly to the passive-cable prediction 2
lambda / tau_m, showing that the optimum is almost independent of dendritic length and is governed
instead by R_m and diameter. This converts a morphology-causal hypothesis into a biophysics-causal
one and shows the biophysics cannot match measured V1 velocity tuning without unphysiological
membrane parameters.

## Datasets

* **32 cat V1 neurons**: extracellularly characterized, HRP-filled, three-dimensionally
  reconstructed with in-house TRAKA system. Cell types: layer 3-6 pyramids (p3-p6), layer 4 star
  pyramids (t4), layer 4 spiny stellates (s4), layer 3 and 4 basket cells (b3, b4), layer 3 double

## Main Ideas

* **A negative cortical benchmark for morphology-to-DS mapping.** In retinal starburst amacrine
  cells, dendritic morphology on its own (radial arbor, distal-versus-proximal synaptic wiring) is
  an essential substrate of direction selectivity. Anderson et al. show the analogous hypothesis
  fails in cat V1 simple cells — our survey must preserve this contrast and never import the SAC
  morphology-causal story wholesale into cortical models.
* **Velocity tuning as a biophysical constraint.** Any morphology-based DS mechanism in a
  compartmental V1 model is pinned by the cable relation **v_opt ~= 2 lambda / tau_m**. If the
  simulated optimal velocity does not match measured V1 tuning (~10-20 degrees/s), the mechanism is
  biophysically implausible regardless of morphology. Our own DS model-fitting should log the
  implied v_opt and compare against species-specific tuning curves.
* **Single-dendrite selectivity collapses under realistic input statistics.** Even the Meynert best
  case loses its velocity peak and its D-P/P-D contrast as soon as inputs are distributed over two
  opposing dendrites. Morphology-only DS mechanisms require almost-deterministic retinotopic mapping
  onto a single dendrite, which is not observed in cortex.
* **Network mechanisms dominate cortical DS.** Recurrent excitation and inhibition in canonical
  cortical microcircuits (Douglas, Martin and colleagues; Suarez et al. 1995; Maex and Orban 1996)
  and LGN-side delay lines from lagged thalamic cells (Saul and Humphrey 1992) must be the principal
  generators of V1 DS — any cortical DS model in this project that ignores these is incomplete.
* **Reusable morphometry pipeline.** The tangential-projection plus 30-degree sector scan at
  variable radial cut-off is directly portable to our DSGC morphology analysis and can be reused as
  a baseline asymmetry metric in the compartmental-model calibration pipeline.

## Summary

Anderson, Binzegger, Kahana, Martin and Segev set out to test, experimentally and computationally,
Livingstone’s 1998 hypothesis that the direction selectivity of neurons in primary visual cortex
arises from tangential asymmetries of the basal dendritic tree acting as a delay line. The question
is central to any theory that links neuronal morphology to direction-selective computation, because
it asks whether the SAC/DSGC morphology-causal story generalizes to neocortex.

Their method has two prongs. Experimentally, they combine in-vivo extracellular and intracellular
recording of 32 cat V1 neurons with post-hoc HRP filling, osmicated resin embedding,
three-dimensional reconstruction and retinotopy-aligned tangential projection, and they measure the
angular offset between each cell’s preferred direction and its dendritic bias. Computationally,
they build a detailed NEURON compartmental model of reconstructed cat and monkey Meynert cells —
the most dendritically asymmetric cortical cells known, with basal dendrites up to 770 micrometres
— in which 2,000 AMPA synapses are swept distal-to-proximal or proximal-to-distal along one or two
dendrites, with a somatic GABA_A synapse providing null-direction shunting inhibition 2 ms after the
nearest excitatory input.

The headline finding is that the morphology-causal hypothesis fails on both prongs. The angular
difference between dendritic bias and preferred direction is uniformly distributed (K-S p = 0.23),
robust across bin sizes and across choices of distal, middle or total dendritic length; only 2 of 32
neurons have their longest dendrite at 180 degrees to preferred, consistent with chance. Even the
Meynert best case produces a velocity-tuned response only at an optimal sweep velocity of ~77
degrees/s — an order of magnitude faster than measured V1 tuning of ~10-20 degrees/s — and this
peak collapses as soon as inputs are mapped onto a second, opposing dendrite. The optimum is set by
the cable relation 2 lambda / tau_m, which is almost independent of dendrite length, closing the
door on the claim that asymmetry magnitude alone rescues the model. The authors conclude that at
most a small fraction of V1 DS can be attributed to single-cell dendritic geometry, and that network
mechanisms — recurrent cortical circuits, lagged-thalamic delay lines, and synaptic timing —
must carry the bulk of the computation.

For this project’s literature survey on morphology-to-DS modelling, the paper is a foundational
negative cortical control. It defines the line between a morphology-causal system (retinal SACs and
DSGCs, where dendritic geometry is a primary substrate of DS) and a network-causal system (V1 simple
cells, where morphology is insufficient). Any compartmental model that we fit against DS tuning
curves must log its implied 2 lambda / tau_m velocity and compare to the Anderson et al. bound; any
cortical morphology-causal claim we encounter in newer papers should be read against this 1999 null
result. The tangential-projection plus polar-sector morphometry pipeline is directly reusable for
our DSGC asymmetry analyses, and the Meynert-cell ModelDB entry (ModelDB 3812) is a concrete
starting point should we later need a cortical compartmental comparator for our retinal DS models.
